Frequently Asked Questions

How is this data collected?

This data is provided by adsb.lol, who collect it from volunteers who receive and share this data. If you're interested in improving coverage near you, you can learn how to feed data to them here.

What is this data good for?

Our hope is that this data is useful for two groups of people:

  • Pilots: so that they can be aware of the locations and types of loss-of-separation events at airports they frequent
  • Also pilots: seeing the (typically non-standard) procedures that lead to events is often eye-opening.
  • Airport owners/managers/officials: so that they can help educate pilots and design procedures that seek to avoid these events when possible
  • Special event operators: ditto!

Has this data ever been used successfully?

Yes! Based on hotspots observed at 88NV in 2022, recommended VFR procedures were adjusted, leading to a ~70% reduction in LOS events in the affected areas 2023, 2024, and 2025.

Why isn't my favorite airport available?

Analyzing all this data is a big job, so initially only the busiest airports in the US are covered. If you want to see an automated analysis for another airport, search for it, then you will be prompted to submit it for analysis. If you are interested in a longer time-range and more detailed report, contact us.

Is this useful for towered airports?

This hasn't been the focus so far. Plenty of operations that are common at towered airports will show up in this analysis. If you have thoughts on this, get in touch.

I see an event that is categorized incorrectly.

This is almost certain to happen given the incomplete nature of the data. But if you see something that you think could be improved algorithmically, by all means get in touch.

The data is noisy.

The data is collected by volunteers and they may not have good reception of the area that you're interested in. See "How is this data collected?" above for how to improve it.

What interesting hotspots have you found so far?

  • Tracy, CA: many events from aircraft attempting straight-in approaches for rwy 30 and 12, including against prevailing traffic.
  • San Martin, CA: Nearby class C airspace creates a high-traffic corridor to the north
  • Deland, FL: hotspot off the end of rwy 30 from users of a crossing runway
  • Shirley, NY: shoreline sightseeing brings a lot of airplanes together
  • Davis, CA: apparently-uncharted ag strip northwest of airport
